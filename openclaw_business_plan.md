## Step 1: Research Your State's LLC Requirements
- Look up your state's LLC filing process
- Find the Secretary of State website for your state
- Check the filing fee (varies by state — could be $50 to $500)
- Check if your state has an annual renewal fee or franchise tax
- Check if your state requires an Operating Agreement (some do, some don't)
- Check if you need a registered agent (some states require it, you can be your own in most)
- Check if you need any local business licenses or permits on top of the LLC
- Write down every requirement and fee so there are no surprises
- **Status: NOT STARTED**

## Step 2: Choose and Reserve the Business Name
- Decide on the exact legal name: "OpenClaw LLC" or "OpenClaw AI LLC" etc.
- Search your state's business name database to make sure it's available
- Search the USPTO trademark database to make sure nobody has trademarked "OpenClaw"
- Search Google to see if there's any other company using "OpenClaw"
- If the name is clear, reserve it with your state (some states let you reserve before filing)
- Check if openclaw.com domain is available — grab it immediately if it is
- If openclaw.com is taken, find alternatives: getopenclaw.com, openclawai.com, etc.
- Buy the domain name right away so nobody takes it ($10-15/yr)
- **Status: NOT STARTED**

## Step 3: File the LLC
- Go to your state's Secretary of State website
- Fill out the Articles of Organization form
- Information you'll need:
  - Business name: OpenClaw LLC
  - Business address (can be your home address to start)
  - Registered agent name and address (can be yourself)
  - Member/manager info (just you for now — single-member LLC)
  - Business purpose (general: "technology services" or "AI consulting and maintenance")
- Pay the filing fee
- Wait for approval (some states are instant online, some take days/weeks)
- Once approved, download and save your Articles of Organization — this is your proof the LLC exists
- **Status: NOT STARTED**

## Step 4: Get an EIN (Employer Identification Number)
- Go to irs.gov — it's 100% free, takes 5 minutes online
- Apply for an EIN for your LLC
- You need this to:
  - Open a business bank account
  - File taxes as a business
  - Look professional to clients and partners
- Save the EIN confirmation letter — you'll need it for the bank
- **Status: NOT STARTED**

## Step 5: Write the Operating Agreement
- Even if your state doesn't require it, WRITE ONE — it protects you
- An Operating Agreement defines:
  - Who owns the company and what percentage (100% you for now)
  - How decisions are made
  - How profits are distributed
  - What happens if you bring on partners later
  - What happens if you want to sell the company
  - Rules for adding/removing members
- Claude helps draft this — then get a quick legal review
- This matters A LOT when you sell equity later (Step 49) — investors want to see this
- Keep it on file — signed and dated
- **Status: NOT STARTED**

## Step 6: Open a Business Bank Account
- Go to a bank (Chase, Bank of America, local credit union, or online bank like Mercury/Relay)
- Bring:
  - Your Articles of Organization
  - Your EIN confirmation letter
  - Government-issued ID
- Open a business checking account under "OpenClaw LLC"
- Get a business debit card
- ALL business money goes through this account — never mix with personal
- Set up online banking so you can track everything easily
- Consider opening a business savings account too (for taxes — set aside 25-30% of revenue)
- **Status: NOT STARTED**

## Step 7: Get a Business Credit Card
- Apply for a business credit card (Capital One Spark, Chase Ink, Amex Blue Business, etc.)
- Use this for ALL business purchases:
  - Mac Minis
  - VPS hosting
  - Domain and email hosting
  - Gas (track mileage separately too)
  - Software subscriptions
  - Business cards printing
- Benefits: builds business credit, earns rewards/cashback, keeps expenses tracked automatically
- Pay it off every month — never carry a balance
- **Status: NOT STARTED**

## Step 8: Research Business Insurance
- You NEED insurance before going into anyone's business and setting up equipment
- **Types to research:**
  - **General Liability Insurance:** covers property damage, bodily injury at client sites
  - **Professional Liability (E&O — Errors & Omissions):** covers you if your service causes a client financial loss (e.g., agent gives wrong info, system goes down and they lose sales)
  - **Cyber Liability Insurance:** covers data breaches — you're handling business data, this is important
- Get quotes from:
  - Hiscox, Next Insurance, The Hartford, State Farm, or local insurance agents
- Compare prices, coverage limits, deductibles
- Pick a plan that covers you without breaking the bank
- This goes into your monthly costs calculation
- **Status: NOT STARTED**

## Step 9: Research Local Business Licenses & Permits
- Some cities/counties require a general business license to operate
- Some require specific permits for IT/technology services
- Check your city and county government websites
- Check if you need a home occupation permit (if working from home)
- File and pay for any required licenses
- Keep all licenses on file — some clients may ask to see them
- **Status: NOT STARTED**

## Step 10: Trademark "OpenClaw"
- File a trademark application with the USPTO (United States Patent and Trademark Office)
- This protects the name "OpenClaw" nationally — nobody else can use it
- Filing fee: ~$250-350 per class
- File under the relevant class for technology/AI services
- This takes months to process, but file it early so it's in the pipeline
- This matters when you scale — you don't want someone in another city using your name
- Claude can help you fill out the application
- **Status: NOT STARTED**

## Step 31: Design the Security Architecture
- You're handling business data — customer info, financial records, inventory
- If data leaks: lawsuits, lost clients, destroyed reputation
- **Security measures to implement:**
  - Full disk encryption on every client Mac Mini (FileVault on macOS)
  - SSH access: key-only authentication, no passwords, ed25519 or RSA 4096-bit keys
  - Firewall configured on every Mac Mini: only allow SSH from your VPS IP
  - Strong unique passwords for Claude accounts (password manager)
  - VPS hardened: fail2ban, firewall, automatic security updates
  - All backups encrypted before upload
  - Access logs: track every SSH session into client machines
- Write this all down as a security checklist that gets followed for every client setup
- **Status: NOT STARTED**

## Step 32: Define Data Handling Procedures
- **What data stays local on the client's Mac Mini:**
  - All business data the agents process
  - Agent activity logs
  - Dashboard data
- **What gets backed up (encrypted):**
  - Agent configurations and prompts
  - Dashboard settings
  - System configs
- **What NEVER leaves the client's machine:**
  - Raw customer data (credit cards, SSN, etc.) — agents shouldn't even have access to this
  - Sensitive financial records — keep these in the client's own systems
- **Data retention:**
  - Active clients: data maintained indefinitely
  - Terminated clients: data deleted within 30 days of termination
  - Backups of terminated clients: deleted within 60 days
- Document all of this — it goes in the privacy policy and service agreement
- **Status: NOT STARTED**

## Step 33: Create an Incident Response Plan
- What happens if there IS a security breach or data leak?
- **Step-by-step incident response:**
  1. Detect the breach (AI monitoring should catch it)
  2. Contain it immediately (shut down affected systems, revoke access)
  3. Assess the damage (what data was exposed, how, who's affected)
  4. Notify the affected client within 24 hours
  5. Fix the vulnerability that caused the breach
  6. Document everything that happened
  7. Implement measures to prevent it from happening again
  8. If required by law, notify relevant authorities
- Having this plan BEFORE you need it = professional, prepared, trustworthy
- **Status: NOT STARTED**
